5. Purpose

Objective: State why this document exists and what decisions it is intended to govern.

This Test Strategy defines the overarching approach, governance model, and technical methods by which quality will be assured across the Genio360 Platform Revamp. It establishes a single point of reference for how testing is planned, executed, measured, and reported across the platform's three access channels — the Installer Portal, the Condo Management Portal, and the Mobile Application — and across the five-tier business hierarchy (Global Admin, Distributor, Installer, Property Management, Guard/Resident) that the platform exists to serve.

The strategy is written to be implementation-agnostic at the level of individual test cases, but implementation-specific at the level of architecture: it assumes a React.js/TypeScript frontend, a Node.js/Express REST API backend, PostgreSQL persistence, JWT-based authentication with RBAC, Docker/Nginx/Azure infrastructure, and GitHub Actions CI/CD, with Playwright (TypeScript) as the automation framework of record.

Why this matters for Genio360 specifically: Because the platform's defining characteristic is its "Funnel Model" of progressive module enablement across hierarchy levels, the test strategy must explicitly account for permission-inheritance testing as a first-class concern, not an incidental one.

6. Scope

Objective: Bound what this strategy governs, distinguishing it from module-level Business Requirement Documents (BRDs) and sprint-level Test Plans.

This strategy governs all test activity performed against the Genio360 platform's three user-facing channels and the supporting API and data layers, across all hierarchy tiers (Global Admin through Resident/Guard). It applies uniformly to functional, non-functional, integration, and regression testing performed by the dedicated QA function, as well as to testing activities performed by developers (unit, component) under the "shift-left" principle described in Section 14.

It does not replace module-specific BRDs, which define functional acceptance criteria at a granular level, nor does it replace sprint Test Plans, which schedule specific test cycles against specific releases. This strategy is the parent document from which those artifacts inherit method, governance, and tooling decisions.

7. Business Context

Objective: Ground the test approach in the commercial and operational reality the platform serves.

Genio360 succeeds the iPlusLiving platform and re-architects it around a multi-tier governance model intended to support FAP's global expansion. Where iPlusLiving operated each property as a largely independent unit, Genio360 introduces Global Admin, Distributor, and Installer layers above Property Management, with module availability cascading downward via the Funnel Model. This is a significant business-risk shift from a testing perspective: defects in hierarchy logic, permission inheritance, or module enablement do not affect a single property — they can silently misconfigure an entire region's worth of properties. The test strategy is therefore deliberately risk-weighted toward hierarchy, access-control, and configuration-cascade scenarios over purely cosmetic UI concerns.

The platform also carries meaningful safety and security exposure through its access-control and visitor-management modules (digital QR access, facial recognition, vehicle ANPR, intercom integrations). Defects here have physical-world consequences — an unauthorised entry or a failed visitor verification is not merely a software bug, it is a building-security incident. This context directly informs the prioritisation rules in Sections 15 and 41–44.

11. Test Objectives

  • Provide objective, metrics-based evidence of release readiness to support Go/No-Go decisions.

13. Test Governance

Objective: Define the decision-making structure and oversight cadence for quality on this program.

Test governance for Genio360 operates at three levels. At the strategic level, the Test Manager/QA Architect owns this document and reports release-readiness status to the Delivery Manager and Product Owner at each sprint review and release checkpoint. At the tactical level, sprint-level Test Plans are reviewed and approved within sprint planning, with entry/exit criteria enforced by the QA Lead embedded in each squad. At the operational level, daily test execution, defect triage, and automation maintenance are managed by QA Engineers against Jira-tracked work items.

Given the hierarchical nature of the product itself, governance mirrors the platform's own structure: a centralised QA policy (this document) is applied locally per squad/module, analogous to how Global Admin policy cascades through Distributor and Installer tiers. Local teams may not weaken entry/exit criteria without QA Architect sign-off, preserving consistency across the program.

14. Testing Principles

Objective: State the working principles that guide day-to-day testing decisions on this program.
  • Shift-left by default: unit and component-level testing is a developer responsibility, validated through CI gates before QA engagement begins.
  • Risk informs effort: test depth and frequency are proportional to business and safety risk, not module size (see Section 15).
  • Automate the regression baseline: any scenario executed more than twice is a candidate for automation; manual testing is reserved for exploratory, usability, and first-time-feature validation.
  • Test the hierarchy, not just the screen: every functional test case for a module must be evaluated against at least two hierarchy tiers to confirm permission boundaries hold.
  • Defects are data: root-cause patterns are reviewed each sprint to feed back into both test design and development practice, not just counted.
  • Testing is a team activity: developers, QA, and Product Owners share accountability for quality; QA is the facilitator and gatekeeper, not the sole owner.

15. Risk-Based Testing Approach

Objective: Define how risk likelihood and impact determine test prioritisation and depth.

Test effort is allocated using a likelihood × impact model, scored per module/feature. Impact is assessed against three lenses specific to Genio360: business impact (does this affect revenue, onboarding, or governance?), safety impact (does this affect physical access control or resident safety?), and blast radius (does a defect here affect one property or cascade across the hierarchy due to the Funnel Model?).

16. Test Scope

Objective: Precisely bound the system under test for this strategy's lifetime.

The scope of testing covers the three Genio360 access channels (Installer Portal, Condo Management Portal, Mobile Application), the underlying REST API layer, the PostgreSQL data layer to the extent it affects functional and data-integrity outcomes, and the defined set of third-party integrations listed in Section 10 of the Business Overview document. Infrastructure-layer testing (Docker, Nginx, Azure configuration) is included where it affects application behaviour (e.g., TLS termination, routing) but excludes general cloud-platform certification, which is the responsibility of the Infrastructure/DevOps function.

18. Features Excluded

  • Legacy iPlusLiving codebase regression — covered under a separate decommissioning validation track, not this strategy.
  • Third-party vendor internal system testing (e.g., internal certification of Hikvision or VIDA Quantum hardware) — limited to interface/contract-level validation only.
  • Marketing/content modules under the i+Living ecosystem (Buy & Sell, Health Reads, Home Services) beyond smoke-level validation, pending confirmation of business priority.
  • Load testing of third-party payment gateway infrastructure itself (Stripe/PayNow/PayLah) — limited to Genio360's integration layer.

19. Assumptions

  • Module-specific BRDs will be made available progressively and reviewed by QA prior to each sprint's test design.
  • Test environments will have representative third-party integration sandboxes (e.g., Stripe test mode, intercom simulators) available before integration testing begins.
  • API contracts (OpenAPI/Swagger) will be maintained and kept current as the source of truth for backend test design.
  • Test data will be synthetic and will not contain production PII at any stage.

20. Constraints

  • Two-week sprint cadence limits the depth of exploratory testing per cycle; risk-based prioritisation (Section 15) is therefore mandatory, not optional.
  • Hardware-dependent integrations (facial recognition cameras, ANPR cameras, physical card readers) cannot be fully exercised in lower environments and require scheduled access to a staging environment with physical hardware.
  • Mobile application testing is constrained by available device lab coverage; a defined device matrix (Section 59) is used to manage this constraint pragmatically.

26. Test Design Techniques

Objective: Define the analytical techniques used to derive test conditions and cases, ensuring systematic rather than ad hoc coverage.
  • Equivalence Partitioning & Boundary Value Analysis — applied to input validation (e.g., unit numbers, visitor PIN length, payment amounts).
  • Decision Table Testing — applied to RBAC and Funnel Model logic, where outcomes depend on combinations of hierarchy tier and module-enablement flags.
  • State Transition Testing — applied to defect workflow, visitor approval workflow, and facility booking lifecycle.
  • Pairwise/Combinatorial Testing — applied to browser/device/role combinations to manage matrix explosion pragmatically.
  • Exploratory Testing — applied to new UI features and usability evaluation, time-boxed and session-chartered.
  • Scenario/Use-Case Based Testing — applied to end-to-end business flows described in Section 8 of the Business Overview (e.g., Distributor onboarding through Resident activation).

28. Test Data Management

Objective: Ensure test data is available, representative, compliant, and repeatable.
  • All non-production environments use synthetic data generated via seed scripts; no production PII is copied downward at any stage.
  • Data sets are structured to represent the full hierarchy (sample Global Admin, Distributor, Installer, Property, Resident, and Guard records) so that permission-boundary tests are always executable.
  • Data refresh is automated as part of environment provisioning in CI/CD, ensuring tests run against a known, version-controlled baseline.
  • Sensitive integration credentials (payment gateway keys, intercom API keys) are managed via secrets storage, never embedded in test data or source control.
Risk: Inconsistent or stale seed data is a common source of false-positive/false-negative automation results. Mitigation: Seed scripts are version-controlled alongside the automation suite and validated as part of the environment-readiness checklist (Section 32).

29. Configuration Management

Test artifacts (test cases, automation scripts, environment configuration) are version-controlled in GitHub alongside application code, ensuring that test suite versions remain traceable to the application version they validate. Branching strategy mirrors the application repository's trunk-based development model, with automation changes reviewed via pull request like any other code change.

30. Build Management

GitHub Actions triggers an automated pipeline on every pull request and merge to main: linting and unit tests run first, followed by component tests, followed by a deployment to the CI ephemeral environment where the Playwright smoke suite executes as a merge gate. Builds that fail any gate are blocked from progressing, enforcing the shift-left principle described in Section 14.

31. Release Management

Releases follow the 2-week sprint cadence, with a release candidate cut at sprint end, deployed to Staging for full regression, then to UAT for business sign-off, before production deployment. Release readiness is formally assessed against the Exit Criteria in Section 33 and the Release Readiness Checklist in Section 71.

32. Entry Criteria

  • Build successfully deployed to the target test environment
  • Smoke test suite passes with zero Critical/High failures
  • Relevant BRD/story acceptance criteria reviewed and understood by QA
  • Test data and environment configuration validated as ready
  • Required third-party sandbox integrations are reachable and authenticated

33. Exit Criteria

  • 100% of planned test cases executed (or formally deferred with risk sign-off)
  • Zero open Critical defects; High-severity defects have an agreed mitigation or fix plan
  • Automated regression suite passes at or above the agreed stability threshold (Section 12)
  • Traceability confirms all in-scope acceptance criteria are covered by at least one test case
  • Test Summary/Exit Report (Section 67) completed and reviewed

34. Suspension Criteria

  • A Critical defect blocks execution of more than 30% of the planned test suite.
  • The test environment becomes unavailable or unstable for more than 4 working hours.
  • A required third-party integration sandbox is unreachable, blocking dependent test cases.
  • Test data corruption is detected that invalidates prior execution results.

35. Resumption Criteria

  • The blocking defect is resolved and verified in a new build, or a viable workaround is agreed and documented.
  • Environment stability is restored and confirmed via a re-run smoke check.
  • Test data integrity is re-validated against the version-controlled baseline.

44. Mitigation Strategy

  • R-01 (RBAC escalation): Dedicated permission-matrix test suite executed on every release; automated negative-path tests asserting denied access for every role/module combination.
  • R-02 (Hardware availability): Scheduled hardware-staging windows booked at sprint start; mocked hardware simulators used for interim coverage.
  • R-03 (Payment integrity): Reconciliation testing against gateway sandbox transaction logs; idempotency and retry-path testing for payment API calls.
  • R-04 (Automation flakiness): Flaky-test quarantine process with weekly triage; retry-with-evidence policy in CI (see Section 51).
  • R-05 (BRD delay): QA participates in backlog refinement to flag testability gaps before sprint commitment.

51. Automation Strategy

Objective: Define what is automated, how, and to what standard, to maximise regression confidence within the 2-week sprint cadence.

Automation is built using Playwright with TypeScript, integrated into GitHub Actions as both a pull-request gate (smoke subset) and a nightly/release regression run (full suite). Candidates for automation are selected using the risk tiers in Section 15: Critical and High-risk modules are automated first and maintained with highest priority.

Cross-browser RegressionPlaywright multi-browser projectsPre-release
Recommendation: Maintain a defined "flaky test budget" — tests exceeding a 2% non-reproducible failure rate over a rolling 10-run window are quarantined and root-caused within the sprint, preventing automation distrust from accumulating.

52. Playwright Framework Architecture

Objective: Define the structural conventions of the automation codebase for maintainability and scale.
  • Page Object Model (POM): each portal (Installer Portal, Condo Management Portal) and key mobile web view maintains its own page-object layer, isolating selectors from test logic.
  • Fixture-based test data: Playwright fixtures inject role-specific authenticated sessions (Global Admin, Distributor, Installer, Property Management, Guard, Resident), enabling rapid cross-role test composition.
  • API-first setup/teardown: test preconditions (e.g., creating a property, enabling a module) are established via direct API calls rather than UI navigation, reducing test runtime and flakiness.
  • Parallel execution & sharding: the suite is sharded across CI runners by module risk tier, ensuring Critical-tier suites report first.
  • Reporting: Playwright's HTML reporter plus a custom CI summary step publishes results to the team's communication channel and Confluence dashboard.

53. API Testing Strategy

Objective: Validate the Express REST API independently of the UI to enable fast, reliable backend confidence.
  • Contract testing against the OpenAPI specification to detect breaking changes before they reach the frontend team.
  • Functional testing of CRUD operations across all hierarchy-scoped endpoints, verifying that responses are correctly scoped to the requesting user's tier (e.g., a Distributor cannot retrieve another Distributor's installer list).
  • Negative testing of authentication and authorisation (expired JWT, missing refresh token, insufficient role) to confirm consistent 401/403 behaviour.
  • Idempotency and error-handling testing for state-changing endpoints (module enablement, payment initiation).
  • API version migration coverage: the iPlus Living platform exposed 6 successive REST API versions across its Yii2 backend; as mobile and integration clients migrate onto the new Node.js/Express API, parallel-run testing compares legacy and new endpoint responses for functionally equivalent operations to catch silent behavioural drift during cutover.
  • Controller-to-route mapping audit: the legacy backend exposed 134 admin controllers; this inventory is used as a checklist during migration test planning to confirm no admin capability is silently dropped when re-platforming to the new Express route layer.

54. Database Testing Strategy

Objective: Confirm data integrity and correctness at the PostgreSQL persistence layer.
  • Schema and migration validation as part of CI, ensuring migrations apply cleanly and are reversible.
  • Referential integrity checks across hierarchy tables (Global Admin → Distributor → Installer → Property → Unit → Resident) to confirm cascading relationships behave as designed.
  • Data-consistency verification between API responses and underlying records, particularly for module-enablement flags central to the Funnel Model.
  • Transaction and rollback testing for multi-step operations (e.g., property onboarding involving multiple related inserts).

55. Security Testing Strategy

Objective: Provide assurance against the security risks most relevant to a multi-tenant, access-control-bearing platform.
  • Authentication testing: JWT expiry handling, refresh-token rotation, session fixation resistance.
  • Authorisation/RBAC testing: systematic verification that every role can access only its permitted modules and data scope, including boundary tests at each hierarchy transition.
  • Input validation and injection testing (SQL injection, XSS) on all user-facing forms, particularly visitor registration and resident profile fields.
  • Sensitive data handling review: PII storage, transmission encryption (TLS), and payment data handling (PCI-relevant flows isolated to gateway tokens, never stored raw).
  • Dependency and container vulnerability scanning integrated into the CI/CD pipeline (Docker image scanning).
Risk: Access-control modules carry physical-security consequences beyond typical application risk. Mitigation: Security testing for these modules is escalated to include a dedicated penetration-test cycle prior to major releases, not just automated scanning.

57. Accessibility Testing Strategy

Resident- and guard-facing surfaces are tested against WCAG 2.1 AA, combining automated scanning (axe-core integrated into Playwright runs) with manual keyboard-navigation and screen-reader spot checks each release. Administrative portals (Installer Portal, Condo Management Portal) are held to a best-effort accessibility standard given their professional, trained-user audience, but automated scanning is still applied to catch egregious issues.

60. Regression Strategy

A full automated regression suite executes nightly against Staging and as a mandatory gate before any production release. Regression scope is risk-weighted: Critical and High-tier modules (Section 15) are regression-tested on every build; Medium and Low-tier modules are regression-tested on a rolling basis to manage execution time within the sprint window.

61. Smoke Testing Strategy

A lightweight smoke suite (typically under 15 minutes execution time) validates core login, navigation, and Critical-tier module availability across all three channels. It runs automatically as a pull-request gate and as the first check after every deployment, providing rapid go/no-go feedback before deeper testing begins.

62. Sanity Testing Strategy

Following a defect fix or minor configuration change, a focused sanity check is performed against the specific affected functionality and its immediate dependencies, rather than the full regression suite. This is typically manual and scoped by the QA Lead based on the defect's affected component, with automation re-run as a follow-up confirmation.

63. UAT Support Strategy

QA supports FAP business stakeholders during UAT by preparing curated, business-representative test data, providing a guided test script aligned to the end-to-end business flows in Section 8 of the Business Overview, and triaging UAT-raised defects alongside the standard defect workflow (Section 39). QA does not execute UAT on the business's behalf but facilitates and observes to capture environment or data issues quickly.

64. Production Validation Strategy

Immediately following production deployment, a restricted smoke suite validates core availability across all three channels without generating production-impacting test data. This is followed by a manual spot-check of the specific features released in that cycle, with rollback criteria pre-agreed as part of the release plan (Section 31).

68. Lessons Learned

A structured retrospective is held at the close of each release, capturing what worked, what didn't, and specific process adjustments. For Genio360 specifically, early releases identified that hierarchy/permission test cases were under-represented relative to UI test cases; this strategy's risk-tiering approach (Section 15) was introduced as a direct response and should itself be revisited if new patterns of escaped defects emerge.

69. Continuous Improvement

  • Quarterly review of the risk register and risk-tier assignments against actual defect-leakage data.
  • Ongoing automation suite health review, retiring obsolete tests and expanding coverage into Medium-tier modules as capacity allows.
  • Periodic test-technique calibration sessions to ensure new QA team members apply the design techniques in Section 26 consistently.
  • Feedback loop from production incidents directly into regression suite additions, closing the gap identified in each Lessons Learned cycle.
